Behavioral Genetics: Causal Effect, R^2, and Omitted Variable Bias

Color-mapped equation walkthrough

ESSGN 2026 Workshop

Model Setup

We start with a simple structural equation:

\[ Y_i = {\color{orange}{a_1X_i}} + {\color{deepskyblue}{e_i}} \]

  • \(\color{orange}{a_1X_i}\): causal part from \(X\) to \(Y\)
  • \(\color{deepskyblue}{e_i}\): residual (everything else)

Assumption for the baseline case: \[ \mathrm{Cov}(X,e)=0 \]

Notation Simplification

To simplify later derivations, define:

\[ \color{orange}{A_i} \equiv \color{orange}{a_1X_i} \]

Then the model is:

\[ Y_i = \color{orange}{A_i} + \color{deepskyblue}{e_i} \]

From this point on, \(\color{orange}{A_i}\) denotes the scaled component from \(\color{orange}{X}\).

Variance Decomposition (Baseline)

From the variance-sum law:

\[ \mathrm{Var}(Y) = {\color{orange}{\mathrm{Var}(A)}} + {\color{deepskyblue}{\mathrm{Var}(e)}} \]

Interpretation:

  • \(\color{orange}{\mathrm{Var}(A)}\) is variance in \(Y\) attributable to \(X\)
  • \(\color{deepskyblue}{\mathrm{Var}(e)}\) is unexplained residual variance

Standardized Share (\(R^2\))

In this one-predictor baseline setup:

\[ R^2 = \frac{{\color{orange}{\mathrm{Var}(A)}}}{\mathrm{Var}(Y)} \]

So \(R^2\) is the share of total variance due to the colored causal term.

From \(\hat{\beta}\) to \(a_1\)

By definition, the estimated slope is:

\[ \hat{\beta} = \frac{\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y)}{\mathrm{Var}(X)} \]

\[ \hat{\beta} = a_1 + {\color{crimson}{\frac{\mathrm{Cov}(X,e)}{\mathrm{Var}(X)}}} \]

So the red term is the correction from the observed regression slope \(\hat{\beta}\) to the causal effect \(a_1\).

Covariance Matrix View (Baseline)

For \((A,e)\), baseline assumption gives:

\[ \Sigma_{(A,e)}= \begin{bmatrix} \mathrm{Var}(A) & {\color{crimson}{0}} \\ {\color{crimson}{0}} & \mathrm{Var}(e) \end{bmatrix} \]

Off-diagonal zero means no correlation between explained and residual components.

What Changes If \(A\) and \(e\) Are Correlated?

Now let:

\[ \mathrm{Cov}(A,e)\neq 0 \]

Then:

\[ \mathrm{Var}(Y) = {\color{orange}{\mathrm{Var}(A)}} + {\color{deepskyblue}{\mathrm{Var}(e)}} + {\color{crimson}{2\mathrm{Cov}(A,e)}} \]

So \(R^2\) is no longer only the pure \(\color{orange}{\mathrm{Var}(A)}\) component.

Biased Regression Coefficient

With \(\mathrm{Cov}(X,e)\neq 0\):

\[ \hat{\beta} = a_1 + {\color{crimson}{\frac{\mathrm{Cov}(X,e)}{\mathrm{Var}(X)}}} \]

Equivalent form:

\[ \hat{\beta} = a_1 + {\color{crimson}{r_{Xe}\frac{\sigma_e}{\sigma_X}}} \]

Meaning:

  • The red term \(\color{crimson}{\frac{\mathrm{Cov}(X,e)}{\mathrm{Var}(X)}}\) is bias from confounding / omitted causes
  • \(\hat{\beta}\) is no longer the causal effect \(a_1\)

Omitted Variable Intuition

Suppose an omitted factor \(U\) affects both \(X\) and \(Y\):

\[ X \leftarrow U \rightarrow Y \]

Then \(U\) leaks into the residual \(e\), creating:

\[ \mathrm{Cov}(X,e)\neq 0 \]

That is exactly the red bias pathway in previous equations.

Takeaway

\[ Y_i={\color{orange}{A_i}}+{\color{deepskyblue}{e_i}} \]

  1. If \(\mathrm{Cov}(A,e)=0\): \(R^2\) cleanly reflects explained variance and \(\hat{\beta}=a_1\).
  2. If \(\mathrm{Cov}(A,e)\neq 0\): covariance enters variance decomposition, and if \(\mathrm{Cov}(X,e)\neq0\), \(\hat{\beta}\) is biased.
  3. In behavioral genetics, this is why causal interpretation needs stronger identification than simple regression.

The Infinitesimal Model for Genetics

Consider a phenotype \(Y\) explained by many SNPs:

\[ {\color{orange}{A_i}} = \sum_{j=1}^{M} {\color{gold}{a_j}} \cdot {\color{lightyellow}{X_{ij}}} \]

  • \(\color{gold}{a_j}\): effect size (weight) of SNP \(j\)
  • \(\color{lightyellow}{X_{ij}}\): genotype of individual \(i\) at SNP \(j\) (coded 0, 1, 2)
  • \(\color{orange}{A_i}\): total additive genetic component

Genetic variance: \[ {\color{orange}{\sigma_A^2}} = \mathrm{Var}(A) = \sum_{j=1}^{M} {\color{gold}{a_j}}^2 \cdot {\color{lightyellow}{\mathrm{Var}(X_j)}} \]

The Full Model with Environment

Phenotype as genetic plus environmental:

\[ Y_i = {\color{orange}{A_i}} + {\color{deepskyblue}{E_i}} \]

Heritability (SNP heritability): \[ h^2 = \frac{{\color{orange}{\sigma_A^2}}}{\mathrm{Var}(Y)} \]

Variance decomposition: \[ \mathrm{Var}(Y) = {\color{orange}{\sigma_A^2}} + {\color{deepskyblue}{\sigma_E^2}} \]

Discovering SNP Effects: GWAS

We don’t know the true effect sizes \(\color{gold}{a_j}\). GWAS estimates them via univariate regression for each SNP:

\[ {\color{plum}{\hat{\beta}_j}} = \frac{\mathrm{Cov}(X_j, Y)}{\mathrm{Var}(X_j)} \]

GWAS test statistic (standardized): \[ {\color{plum}{z_j}} = \frac{{\color{plum}{\hat{\beta}_j}}}{\mathrm{SE}({\color{plum}{\hat{\beta}_j}})} \]

  • \(\color{plum}{z_j}\) measures signal strength for SNP \(j\)
  • But these estimates are confounded by Linkage Disequilibrium (LD)

What is Linkage Disequilibrium (LD)?

LD: SNPs are in non-random association in the population.

Two SNPs \(j\) and \(k\) are in LD if: \[ \mathrm{Cov}(X_j, X_k) \neq 0 \]

Examples of LD: - Adjacent SNPs on chromosome (physical proximity) - SNPs in low recombination regions - Recent mutations on same haplotype background

Why Does LD Occur Locally?

SNPs that are close together on the genome tend to be inherited together.

Genetic basis: - Recombination is rare over short distances - During meiosis, DNA segments swap between chromosomes - Segments inherited intact from parent → SNPs stay together

Physical proximity → genetic linkage → LD

Two SNPs on the same chromosome → correlated genotypes → \(\mathrm{Cov}(X_j, X_k) \neq 0\)

The LD Confounding Problem

When we regress \(Y\) on SNP \(j\), we also capture the effects transmitted through other SNPs in LD with \(j\):

\[ {\color{plum}{\hat{\beta}_j}} = {\color{gold}{a_j}} + \sum_{k \neq j} {\color{gold}{a_k}} \cdot {\color{limegreen}{\rho_{jk}}} \]

Components: - \(\color{gold}{a_j}\): true effect of SNP \(j\) (what we want) - \(\sum_{k \neq j} {\color{gold}{a_k}} \cdot {\color{limegreen}{\rho_{jk}}}\): confounding bias from SNPs in LD - \(\color{limegreen}{\rho_{jk}}\): LD correlation between SNPs \(j\) and \(k\)

Key problem: The estimated \(\color{plum}{\hat{\beta}_j}\) mixes SNP \(j\)’s true effect with effects of its LD partners.

Linkage Disequilibrium Score (LD-score)

Define the LD-score for SNP \(j\):

\[ {\color{limegreen}{\ell_j}} = \sum_{k=1}^{M} \rho_{jk}^2 \]

  • Sum of squared LD correlations between SNP \(j\) and (nearby) other SNPs
  • Measures how much LD “burden” SNP \(j\) carries

Practical limitation - Local LD:

Computationally, we cannot compute correlations between each SNP and all millions of other SNPs. Instead, LD-scores are calculated using only local LD (SNPs in a genomic window around the target SNP). This is computationally feasible and captures the dominant LD structure.

Interpretation: - Minimum LD-score = 1: Each SNP is correlated with itself (\(\rho_{jj}^2 = 1\)) - SNPs with no additional LD have \(\ell_j = 1\) - High LD-score \(\rightarrow\) SNP \(j\) is in strong local LD with many other SNPs (e.g., \(\ell_j = 5\) means it tags 5 SNPs total including itself)

How LD-scores Are Computed

The Test Statistic Pattern

GWAS test statistics show a striking pattern. They scale with LD and heritability:

\[ {\color{plum}{z_j}}^2 \propto {\color{limegreen}{\ell_j}} \cdot {\color{orange}{h^2}} + \text{noise} \]

More precisely (on average across SNPs): \[ \mathbb{E}[{\color{plum}{z_j}}^2] \approx N \left( {\color{limegreen}{\ell_j}} \cdot {\color{orange}{h^2}} + 1 \right) \]

Understanding the Test Statistic Equation

Breaking down the components:

\[ \mathbb{E}[{\color{plum}{z_j}}^2] \approx N \left( {\color{limegreen}{\ell_j}} \cdot {\color{orange}{h^2}} + 1 \right) \]

  • \(\color{limegreen}{\ell_j}\): LD-score of SNP \(j\) (includes correlation with itself + other SNPs in LD)
  • \(\color{orange}{h^2}\): SNP heritability per SNP (true signal strength)
  • \(N\): sample size
  • The “+1” term: from each SNP’s perfect self-correlation (minimum LD-score is 1)

Key insight: Test statistics scale linearly with LD-score: - A SNP with no LD (\(\ell_j = 1\)) has expected \(z_j^2 \approx N(h^2 + 1)\) - A SNP with 5x LD burden (\(\ell_j = 5\)) has expected \(z_j^2 \approx N(5 h^2 + 1)\)

We will exploit this relationship in LD-score regression.

The Logic of LD-score Regression

Confounding by LD: We observe \(\color{plum}{z_j}^2\) for many SNPs, and they’re confounded by LD.

Exploit the confounding: Since test statistics scale linearly with LD-score, we can use this relationship to estimate heritability:

\[ \mathbb{E}[{\color{plum}{z_j}}^2] = {\color{orange}{\tau_h}} \cdot {\color{limegreen}{\ell_j}} + {\color{purple}{\tau_0}} \]

  • \(\color{orange}{\tau_h}\) (slope): heritability signal per unit LD
  • \(\color{purple}{\tau_0}\) (intercept): non-heritability contribution (polygenicity, population structure, etc.)

LD-score Regression: The Method

We exploit the LD relationship to estimate heritability:

Step-by-step approach: 1. For each SNP \(j\), compute \(\color{limegreen}{\ell_j}\) (local LD-score) 2. Regress observed \(\color{plum}{z_j}^2\) on \(\color{limegreen}{\ell_j}\) across all SNPs 3. From regression slope \(\Rightarrow\) estimate \(\color{orange}{h^2}\) (heritability) 4. From intercept \(\Rightarrow\) assess confounding/inflation from other sources

LD-score Regression Formulas

Linear regression model:

\[ {\color{plum}{z_j}}^2 = {\color{purple}{\text{intercept}}} + {\color{orange}{\text{slope}}} \cdot {\color{limegreen}{\ell_j}} + \epsilon_j \]

where: - \(\color{purple}{\text{intercept}}\): non-genetic sources of inflation (structure, polygenicity artifacts) - \(\color{orange}{\text{slope}}\): heritability signal (proportional to \(h^2\)) - \(\color{limegreen}{\ell_j}\): local LD-score (captures the confounding pattern) - \(\epsilon_j\): residual error

Computing Heritability from LD-score Regression

From the regression slope, we extract heritability:

\[ \widehat{{\color{orange}{h^2}}} = \frac{{\color{orange}{\text{slope}}} \times N}{\text{# SNPs}} \]

This estimates SNP heritability by exploiting the relationship between test statistics and LD-score.

Advantages of LD-score Regression

Why LD-score regression exploits the confounding:

  • Naive approach: \(\hat{h}^2 \propto \sum {{\color{plum}{z_j}}}^2\)
    Problem: High-LD SNPs contribute more and inflate estimates
  • LD-score regression: Use the linear relationship \(z_j^2 \propto {{\color{limegreen}{\ell_j}}} \cdot {{\color{orange}{h^2}}}\)
    Advantage: Extracts \({{\color{orange}{h^2}}}\) by accounting for LD variation
    Robust to polygenicity and LD patterns

Summary: From GWAS to LD-score Regression

The complete picture:

  1. Infinitesimal model: \(Y = \sum {\color{gold}{a_j}} X_j + {\color{deepskyblue}{E}}\)
  2. GWAS estimates: \({{\color{plum}{\hat{\beta}_j}}}\) for each SNP (test statistics \({{\color{plum}{z_j}}}\))
  3. Confounding: LD causes \({{\color{plum}{z_j}}}^2 \propto {{\color{limegreen}{\ell_j}}} \cdot {{\color{orange}{h^2}}}\)
  4. LD-score regression: Exploit this relationship by regressing \({{\color{plum}{z_j}}}^2\) on \({{\color{limegreen}{\ell_j}}}\)
  5. Extraction: From slope estimate \({{\color{orange}{h^2}}}\)
  6. Insight: LD confounds individual SNP estimates but enables heritability recovery via LDSC
